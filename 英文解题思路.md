**Mathematical Model for Maximizing Annual Electricity Generation of a Central Receiver Solar Power Plant**

---

## **1. Coordinate System and Variables**

We define a 3D Cartesian coordinate system:
- Origin \( O \): base of the central receiver tower (center of the heliostat field).
- \( z \)-axis: vertical upward; receiver aperture center located at \( T = (0, 0, H_t) \).
- Heliostat field: circular region centered at the origin.
- Heliostat \( i \): center coordinates \( M_i = (x_i, y_i, 6) \), mirror size \( 6 \times 6 \, \text{m} \).

Sun position angles:
- Solar altitude \( \alpha_s(t) \) (elevation above horizontal).
- Solar azimuth \( \gamma_s(t) \) (measured clockwise from North, 0° to 360°).

Heliostat orientation angles:
- Elevation/tilt angle \( \theta_i(t) \) (angle between mirror normal and horizontal plane).
- Azimuth angle \( \phi_i(t) \) (projection of mirror normal onto horizontal plane, measured clockwise from North).

---

## **2. Reflection Geometry and Mirror Normal Vector**

Unit vector pointing from Earth to Sun (sun direction vector):
\[
\mathbf{\hat{s}}_{\text{sun}} = 
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\alpha_s \sin\gamma_s \\
\cos\alpha_s \cos\gamma_s \\
\sin\alpha_s
\end{bmatrix}
\]
(Convention: \( x \)-axis East, \( y \)-axis North, \( z \)-axis Up.)

Incident ray direction (from sun to mirror) is \( \mathbf{i} = -\mathbf{\hat{s}}_{\text{sun}} \).

Reflected ray direction (from mirror to target):
\[
\mathbf{\hat{r}} = \frac{T - M_i}{\| T - M_i \|}
\]

Mirror unit normal vector (using reflection law with incident vector pointing toward mirror):
\[
\mathbf{\hat{n}} = \frac{\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{\hat{r}}}{\| \mathbf{i} + \mathbf{\hat{r}} \|}
\]
This ensures \( \mathbf{\hat{r}} = \mathbf{i} - 2(\mathbf{i} \cdot \mathbf{\hat{n}})\mathbf{\hat{n}} \) holds.

From \( \mathbf{\hat{n}} = (n_x, n_y, n_z) \), the mirror orientation angles are:
\[
\theta_i = \arcsin(n_z), \quad \phi_i = \operatorname{atan2}(n_x, n_y)
\]
(using atan2 for correct quadrant, result in radians clockwise from North).

---

## **3. Effective Area and Cosine Loss**

Mirror area: \( A_m = 36 \, \text{m}^2 \).

Cosine efficiency (projected area toward sun):
\[
\eta_{\cos} = \mathbf{\hat{n}} \cdot (-\mathbf{\hat{s}}_{\text{sun}}) = \mathbf{\hat{n}} \cdot \mathbf{i}
\]
This is the cosine of the angle between mirror normal and incident sunlight.

---

## **4. Solar Position Model**

Solar declination (day number \( n \), 1 to 365):
\[
\delta \approx 23.45^\circ \times \sin\left( \frac{2\pi (n - 80)}{365} \right) \quad \text{(in degrees)}
\]

Hour angle:
\[
h = 15^\circ \times (\text{solar time} - 12)
\]
(Solar time = local time + EOT correction; for simplicity, EOT may be neglected initially.)

Solar altitude:
\[
\sin \alpha_s = \sin \varphi \, \sin \delta + \cos \varphi \, \cos \delta \, \cos h
\]
Solar azimuth (from North clockwise):
\[
\cos \gamma_s = \frac{\sin \delta \cos \varphi - \cos \delta \sin \varphi \cos h}{\cos \alpha_s}, 
\quad \sin \gamma_s = \frac{\cos \delta \sin h}{\cos \alpha_s}
\]
\[
\gamma_s = \operatorname{atan2}(\sin \gamma_s, \cos \gamma_s) \quad \text{(adjust quadrant if needed)}
\]

---

## **5. Energy Flux and Efficiency Factors**

**Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI)** \( I_{\text{dni}}(t) \):  
Simplified clear-sky model (e.g., Hottel’s) or use typical meteorological year (TMY) data.

**Reflectivity**: \( \rho \approx 0.88 \) (typical mirror reflectance).

**Atmospheric attenuation** (between mirror and receiver):
\[
\eta_{\text{atm}} = a^{d}, \quad a \approx 0.99 \ \text{per km}, \quad d = \| M_i - T \| / 1000 \ \text{(km)}
\]

**Truncation efficiency** \( \eta_{\text{trunc}} \): fraction of reflected cone entering receiver aperture. Depends on mirror shape, aiming, distance, and receiver size. Simplified: if receiver fully captures the reflected beam, \( \eta_{\text{trunc}} = 1 \); otherwise computed via cone interception.

**Blocking/shading loss** \( \eta_{\text{block}} \): neglected here (\(=1\)) for simplicity, but in detailed layout optimization, it must be modeled.

Instantaneous power reflected by heliostat \( i \):
\[
P_i(t) = I_{\text{dni}}(t) \cdot A_m \cdot \rho \cdot \eta_{\cos} \cdot \eta_{\text{atm}} \cdot \eta_{\text{trunc}}
\]

Total thermal power to receiver:
\[
P_{\text{field}}(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} P_i(t)
\]

---

## **6. Annual Energy to Electricity**

Thermal-to-electric conversion efficiency: \( \eta_{\text{th}} \approx 0.35 \).

Annual electricity generated:
\[
E_{\text{year}} = \eta_{\text{th}} \int_{\text{year}} P_{\text{field}}(t) \, dt
\]
Integration over daylight hours when \( \alpha_s > 0 \) and heliostat not in maintenance/night.

Average annual electric power:
\[
\overline{P}_{\text{electric}} = \frac{E_{\text{year}}}{8760 \ \text{hours}}
\]

---

## **7. Maximization Approach**

Given fixed heliostat positions, the only control is aiming law (already set by reflection geometry).  
Maximization of annual output is achieved by:
- Optimal layout to minimize average attenuation and cosine losses.
- Ensuring \( \eta_{\text{trunc}} \approx 1 \) by proper receiver sizing.
- In this model, we compute \( P_{\text{field}}(t) \) for all \( t \), then integrate.

No additional degrees of freedom once coordinates and aiming strategy are fixed.
